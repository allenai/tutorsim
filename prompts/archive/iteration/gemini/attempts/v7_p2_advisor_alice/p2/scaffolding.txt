## Scaffolding (Push for Rigor vs. Support)

You are analyzing the tradeoff tutors face between making a task accessible (scaffolding) and maintaining cognitive demand (pushing for rigor).

- **Push for Rigor**: Challenging the student, asking for explanations, requiring metacognition, and removing support to foster independence.
- **Scaffold**: Breaking problems into steps, providing hints, modeling, using visuals, or simplifying the task to address a struggle.

Types of moments:

1. **Effective Scaffolding/Rigor**: The tutor correctly identifies the student's need and responds with an appropriate strategy. If the student is stuck, the tutor provides just enough support. If the student is coasting, the tutor increases the challenge.

2. **Missed Opportunity (Ineffective)**:
   - **Missed Scaffold**: Student is stuck, but the tutor offers no help or repeats the same ineffective strategy.
   - **Missed Rigor (difficulty gap)**: Student solves problems easily, but the tutor keeps the task too simple.
   - **Missed Rigor (depth gap)**: Student demonstrates thinking beyond what was asked, but the tutor responds with generic praise and moves on.

## Effectiveness Criteria

*Note on Cognitive Load & Mathematical Soundness: A scaffold is only effective or partial if it is mathematically sound and manages cognitive load. If a strategy overloads the student's working memory with unrelated calculations, or uses an inefficient method (e.g., using repeated addition instead of introducing multiplication for equal groups), it is ineffective.*

- **effective**: The tutor reads the student's level correctly and responds with an appropriate, well-executed strategy. The scaffold is calibrated to the student's need — not too much, not too little. Modeling a process for a genuinely stuck student is a legitimate scaffold, not a failure.

- **partial**: The tutor's strategy helps but is incomplete: (a) the student gets the answer but understanding is unclear, (b) the tutor mixes good questioning with telling, (c) the tutor directs every step of a mathematically sound process — student computes but doesn't decide what to do next, or (d) the approach was mathematically sound and did not overload the student, but missed the specific misconception.

- **ineffective**: Telling instead of asking when the student could reason it out, not adapting to the student's misconception, introducing unnecessary complexity that overloads working memory, using a mathematically inefficient or flawed approach (e.g., missing the core concept like multiplication), scaffolding when the student doesn't need help, missing an obvious opportunity to push for deeper thinking, or the student remains stuck despite repeated attempts.
