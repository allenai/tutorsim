## Scaffolding (Push for Rigor vs. Support)

You are analyzing the tradeoff tutors face between making a task accessible (scaffolding) and maintaining cognitive demand (pushing for rigor).

- **Push for Rigor**: Challenging the student, asking for explanations, requiring metacognition, and removing support to foster independence.
- **Scaffold**: Breaking problems into steps, providing hints, modeling, using visuals, or simplifying the task to address a struggle.

Types of moments:

1. **Effective Scaffolding/Rigor**: The tutor correctly identifies the student's need and responds with an appropriate strategy. If the student is stuck, the tutor provides just enough support. If the student is coasting, the tutor increases the challenge.

2. **Missed Opportunity (Ineffective)**:
   - **Missed Scaffold**: Student is stuck, but the tutor offers no help or repeats the same ineffective strategy.
   - **Missed Rigor (difficulty gap)**: Student solves problems easily, but the tutor keeps the task too simple.
   - **Missed Rigor (depth gap)**: Student demonstrates thinking beyond what was asked, but the tutor responds with generic praise and moves on.

## Diagnostic Calibration

Effective tutoring begins with correctly diagnosing WHERE the student is. The same mathematical topic spans multiple grade levels, and the right scaffold depends on the student's current understanding — not the grade-level expectation.

- **Switching to direct instruction for prerequisite gaps is a sophisticated choice, not a failure to scaffold.** If a student is working on quadratics but cannot define "factoring," pausing to directly teach the prerequisite is the right move.
- **Forgot vs. never learned**: A student who once knew a concept but has forgotten needs a brief refresher. A student who never learned it needs more substantial instruction. Correctly diagnosing which applies is a mark of effective teaching.
- **Sense-making before procedures**: A tutor who asks "what is happening in this problem?" before "what is the next step?" is grounding the student in meaning. This is effective scaffolding even if it looks like the tutor is not directly helping with the math.
- **Vocabulary precision as scaffolding**: A tutor who insists on precise mathematical terms (e.g., correcting "the highest point" to "the vertex") is building the student's capacity for harder problems. This is effective, not pedantic.

A strategy can be effective even if the student's visible response is brief — some students show understanding through correct answers rather than verbal elaboration. Conversely, a strategy can be ineffective even if the student gets the right answer — if the tutor did all the thinking.

## Effectiveness Criteria

- **effective**: The tutor reads the student's level correctly and responds with an appropriate, well-executed strategy. The scaffold is calibrated to the student's need — not too much, not too little. Modeling a process for a genuinely stuck student is a legitimate scaffold, not a failure. Switching to direct instruction for a student who lacks prerequisite knowledge is a correct diagnostic choice.

- **partial**: The tutor's strategy helps but is incomplete: (a) the student gets the answer but understanding is unclear, (b) the tutor mixes good questioning with telling, (c) the tutor directs every step — student computes but doesn't decide what to do next, or (d) the approach was reasonable but missed the specific misconception.

- **ineffective**: Telling instead of asking when the student could reason it out, not adapting to the student's misconception, scaffolding when the student doesn't need help, missing an obvious opportunity to push for deeper thinking, or the student remains stuck despite repeated attempts with the same strategy.
