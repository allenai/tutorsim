## Scaffolding (Push for Rigor vs. Support)

You are analyzing the tradeoff tutors face between making a task accessible (scaffolding) and maintaining cognitive demand (pushing for rigor).

- **Push for Rigor**: Challenging the student, asking for explanations, requiring metacognition, and removing support to foster independence.
- **Scaffold**: Breaking problems into steps, providing hints, modeling, using visuals, or simplifying the task to address a struggle.

Types of moments:

1. **Effective Scaffolding/Rigor**: The tutor correctly identifies the student's need and responds with an appropriate strategy. If the student is stuck, the tutor provides just enough support. If the student is coasting, the tutor increases the challenge.

2. **Missed Opportunities**:
   - **Missed Scaffold (Ineffective)**: Student is stuck, but the tutor offers no help, ignores the misconception, or repeats the same ineffective strategy.
   - **Preemptive / Over-Scaffolding (Partial/Ineffective)**: The tutor jumps in to break the problem into micro-steps or explicitly model the process *before* giving the student a chance to attempt it independently, robbing them of productive struggle. (Ineffective if the tutor takes over all sense-making; Partial if the student still does some meaningful work).
   - **Routine Missed Rigor (Ineffective)**: Student solves problems easily, but the tutor just accepts the answers with generic praise and moves on without asking "how" or "why" to verify understanding.
   - **Incomplete Rigor (Partial)**: Student solves problems easily and the tutor asks for basic reasoning, but misses an obvious opportunity to extend the concept or push deeper.
   - **Extreme Missed Rigor (Ineffective)**: Student explicitly demonstrates deep thinking or asks a conceptual question, but the tutor completely ignores it and moves on.

## Effectiveness Criteria

- **effective**: The tutor reads the student's level correctly and responds with an appropriate strategy. The scaffold is calibrated to the student's need. Breaking a complex problem into highly directed micro-steps or explicitly modeling a process is highly effective ONLY IF the student has first been given a chance to try and is genuinely stuck. Clearing minor roadblocks (e.g., defining a vocabulary word, reading a large number, or doing a minor arithmetic step) is an effective scaffold if it unblocks the student to focus on the main concept.

- **partial**: The tutor's strategy is okay but flawed: (a) the student gets the answer but understanding is unclear, (b) the tutor provides useful guidance but does slightly too much heavy lifting on the *core concept* or jumps in a bit too early with scaffolds before assessing independent ability, (c) the approach was reasonable but missed the core misconception, or (d) the tutor asks for basic reasoning but misses an obvious chance to extend a coasting student's understanding (incomplete rigor).

- **ineffective**: Routine missed rigor (student correctly answers easily and tutor just accepts it and moves on without asking them to justify or explain), preemptively telling the student exactly what to do or breaking it into micro-steps before giving them a chance to try, taking over all the sense-making so the student learns nothing, ignoring a student's explicit attempt to think deeper, abruptly jumping to a much harder unintroduced topic, or the student remains stuck despite repeated attempts.
