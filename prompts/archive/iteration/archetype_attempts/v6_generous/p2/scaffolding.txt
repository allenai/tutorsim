## Scaffolding (Push for Rigor vs. Support)

You are analyzing the tradeoff tutors face between making a task accessible (scaffolding) and maintaining cognitive demand (pushing for rigor).

- **Push for Rigor**: Challenging the student, asking for explanations, requiring metacognition, and removing support to foster independence.
- **Scaffold**: Breaking problems into steps, providing hints, modeling, using visuals, or simplifying the task to address a struggle.

Types of moments:

1. **Effective Scaffolding/Rigor**: The tutor correctly identifies the student's need and responds with an appropriate strategy. If the student is stuck, the tutor provides just enough support. If the student is coasting, the tutor increases the challenge.

2. **Missed Opportunity (Ineffective)**:
   - **Missed Scaffold**: Student is stuck, but the tutor offers no help or repeats the same ineffective strategy.
   - **Missed Rigor (difficulty gap)**: Student solves problems easily, but the tutor keeps the task too simple.
   - **Missed Rigor (depth gap)**: Student demonstrates thinking beyond what was asked, but the tutor responds with generic praise and moves on. (Note: It is perfectly acceptable and "effective" to simply confirm a correct answer and move on for standard practice problems. Do not penalize tutors for failing to ask "why?" or "how did you get that?" after every correct answer unless the student explicitly signals a deeper insight).

## Using Context

The surrounding turns provide context for your analysis. If the turns after the detected moment show evidence of the strategy's impact, you must factor this into your effectiveness rating. If a pedagogical move sounds good in theory but leaves the student visibly confused, guessing randomly, or highly distracted, it was poorly executed for that specific student and is not 'effective'. A strategy is only effective if it actually helps the student progress or meaningfully engage with the math.

A strategy can be effective even if the student's visible response is brief — some students show understanding through correct answers rather than verbal elaboration. Conversely, a strategy can be ineffective even if the student gets the right answer — if the tutor did all the thinking.

## Effectiveness Criteria

- **effective**: The tutor reads the student's level correctly and responds appropriately. If the student is stuck, confused, or learning a new topic, heavy scaffolding is **Effective**. This includes breaking the problem down into simple micro-computations (e.g., 'What is 9 minus 4?'), explicitly directing the student's attention ('Look at the y-coordinates'), or setting up the equation for them. Do not penalize the tutor for 'doing the conceptual thinking' or 'reducing the problem to basic arithmetic' if they are guiding a struggling student through a new or difficult process. Modeling and direct instruction are valid, highly effective scaffolds here. Additionally, if a tutor gives away the answer or confirms a guess but immediately follows up with 'Why?' or 'How do you know?' to push for reasoning, this is an **effective** strategy.

- **partial**: The tutor's strategy helps but is flawed. Examples: (a) the tutor provides a reasonable scaffold but misses the core misconception, (b) the student arrives at the correct answer but their conceptual understanding remains unclear, or (c) the tutor provides slightly too much scaffolding on a new problem, but the student still actively engages and benefits. Do NOT penalize a tutor with 'partial' just because they gave a strong hint, supplied vocabulary, or did some telling, as long as it successfully helped a struggling student move forward.

- **ineffective**: Over-scaffolding: the tutor jumps in to heavily scaffold, explain the strategy, or give the answer **before** the student has had a chance to attempt the problem or state what they know. Over-scaffolding a task the student has **already practiced successfully** in previous turns (e.g., walking them step-by-step through a process they should now do independently). Doing all the cognitive work so the student becomes a passive observer (Note: explicitly modeling or writing steps is acceptable and **effective** if the student is visibly stuck, confused, or has asked for help). Or, completely missing an obvious opportunity to address a misconception.
