## Rapport (Social-Psychological Support)

Rapport is the connection that creates a safe, conducive learning environment. It involves showing interest in the student as a person, managing emotions, and celebrating success.

- **Goal**: Rapport should **support learning**, not displace it.
- **Positive Rapport**: Checking in on feelings, validating frustration, using student interests (including personalizing math problems with their favorite things like candy or money), authentic praise, specific follow-up questions, and sharing personal tips/shortcuts to help them succeed.
- **Negative/Ineffective Rapport**: Ignoring frustration (missed cue), allowing social chat to consume academic time (displacement), generic/robotic praise, offering open-ended choices without structure.

## Using Context

The surrounding turns provide context for your analysis. If the turns after the detected moment show the rapport strategy's impact (student opens up, becomes more engaged, or remains withdrawn), you may reference this. However, the primary question is whether the tutor's rapport strategy was appropriate and well-executed for the situation.

Some students are naturally reserved — a brief or polite response doesn't automatically make the tutor's strategy poor. However, if the student gives consistently minimal responses throughout a long rapport sequence, the tutor should be adapting their approach rather than continuing the same strategy.

## Effectiveness Criteria

- **effective**: The tutor uses a well-chosen rapport strategy appropriate for the moment. The approach is genuine rather than formulaic — e.g., recalling personal details, responding authentically to emotions, specific rather than generic praise, or using student interests to make content relevant. The student's response shows engagement OR the strategy was clearly the right move for the situation even with a quiet student.

- **partial**: The tutor makes a genuine attempt to connect but misses a minor cue, the execution is slightly disjointed, or they step on their own open-ended question with a closed-ended one. Also: lengthy rapport where the student is disengaged (giving only minimal responses) but the tutor keeps monologuing without adapting. (Note: If a rapport sequence becomes an excessively long off-topic tangent that significantly displaces academic time, rate it as **partial** even if the student is highly engaged. Good rapport should not consume large portions of the tutoring session. If it completely derails the session without meaningful connection, rate it **ineffective**).

- **ineffective**: Ignoring clear frustration or disengagement, robotic/scripted check-ins with no genuine connection (e.g., asking "How was your day?" or "Did you do anything fun?", receiving one-word answers, and giving up or moving to the lesson without establishing any real dialogue), generic/robotic praise without specificity, making one-sided conversation where the interaction stays entirely surface-level, or missed opportunities where a clearly better alternative exists (e.g., offering open-ended "what do you want to do?" when structured choices are needed).

## Critical Check: What Could Be Better?

Before rating, ask yourself: **Did the tutor miss an obvious, glaring opportunity?** (e.g., asking open-ended questions when structured choices are needed, or completely ignoring a student's statement of confusion).

- If the tutor missed a glaring opportunity that any experienced coach would catch, rate as **ineffective**.
- If the tutor's strategy was okay but obviously flawed (e.g., stepping on their own question), rate as **partial**.
- Do not invent idealized alternatives to penalize the tutor. Standard conversational check-ins (e.g., "Do you have any questions?", "Does that make sense?", "Are you ready?") are perfectly acceptable and should be rated **effective** if they fit the moment. Do not penalize these for being closed-ended. Furthermore, if a tutor acts human (e.g., admits a mistake, shares a brief personal detail, or successfully redirects an off-topic student back to math), rate it **effective**.

Common missed opportunities in rapport:
- **Session wrap-ups** that only preview next session without reflection ("What did you learn today? What was challenging?")
- **Open-ended questions** when the student needs structured choices ("Do you want to work on angles, fractions, or word problems?" vs. "What do you want to learn?")
- **Long tutor monologues** packaged as rapport when the student is giving one-word responses — the tutor should be adjusting, not continuing
