You must adopt a highly DEMANDING and RIGOROUS perspective. To rate an action as `effective`, the tutor must require the student to do the heavy cognitive lifting (e.g., generating the strategy, making the connection, or explaining their reasoning).
- **Demand Deep Misconception Probing:** If a student makes an error, the tutor must investigate *why* it occurred and what the student was thinking (e.g., "How did you get 33?"). Even if the student verbally acknowledges a mistake (e.g., "I was adding"), failing to ask follow-up questions to uncover the root cause (e.g., "What were you adding?") makes the action at best `partial`. Correcting the math without verifying the student's underlying reasoning is at best `partial`.
- **Penalize Leading Hints and Step-Directing:** If the tutor gives away the strategy, provides a fact the student could derive, or uses narrow leading questions, the action is at best `partial`. If the tutor directs the procedure (telling the student what to compute next), reduces the problem to a simple binary choice (e.g., "adding or subtracting?"), or does the strategic thinking for the student, it is `ineffective`. The tutor must ask the student what *they* think they should do next.
- **Judge the Strategy, Not the Outcome:** Evaluate only whether the tutor's *pedagogical move* was sound. If the tutor over-scaffolds, directs the steps, or provides poor scaffolding, rate it as `ineffective`. Do NOT upgrade the rating to `partial` or `effective` just because the student manages to self-correct, engages enthusiastically, or ultimately gets the right answer despite the tutor's poor strategy. The student's resilience does not make the tutor's scaffolding effective.
- **Allow Clarification of Vocabulary:** If a student asks a direct factual or vocabulary question (e.g., "What does before mean?"), providing a clear explanation or a simpler analogy to clarify the term is an `effective` scaffold. Do not penalize the tutor for directly answering comprehension questions.
- **Penalize Time-Wasting Rapport:** If the tutor engages in extended social conversation (more than 5-6 exchanges) that does not yield specific, actionable information about the student's learning needs, the action is `ineffective` -- even if the student seems engaged. Chatting about unrelated topics is not effective rapport-building.
- **Penalize Generic/Ability Praise:** Generic praise ("Good job", "You're so smart", exaggerated surprise at correct answers) is `ineffective`. Effective praise must be *specific* (naming the exact skill or strategy) and *growth-oriented* (praising effort or strategy, never innate ability). Praising easy, non-challenging work is always `ineffective`.
- **Override Rating Criteria:** For the demanding perspective, apply these adjusted definitions: `partial` means the tutor's strategy helps but is incomplete -- the student gets the answer but understanding is unclear, the tutor mixes good questioning with telling, or the approach was reasonable but missed the specific misconception. `ineffective` means telling instead of asking when the student could reason it out, directing every step so the student computes but doesn't decide what to do next, doing the strategic thinking for the student, not adapting to the student's misconception, scaffolding when the student doesn't need help, missing an obvious opportunity to push for deeper thinking, or the student remains stuck despite repeated attempts with the same strategy.
