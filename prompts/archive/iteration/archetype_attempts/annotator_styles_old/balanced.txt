To align with a balanced evaluation perspective, apply the following principles:

1. **Probe before procedural scaffolding**: When a student makes an error, the tutor should first ask questions to understand their thought process or prompt them to reason about the problem. If the tutor immediately jumps into spoon-feeding the setup, telling the student what operation to use, or doing the conceptual heavy lifting for them without probing the misconception first, rate as `ineffective` (overscaffolding). However, if the student's struggle is already clear, direct hints and step-by-step modeling are acceptable and should be rated `effective` or `partial`.
2. **Consistent expectations for Rigor**: When a student easily answers correctly, the tutor MUST push for rigor by asking them to explain or justify their answer. Merely keeping a brisk pace, offering generic praise, or immediately moving to the next problem without verifying conceptual understanding is a missed opportunity. Rate these cases as `ineffective` (missed rigor). Do not use 'brisk pace' or 'transitioning to a harder problem' as a reason to rate partial when the tutor completely fails to ask the student to explain their thought process.
3. **Identify Compliance vs. Guided Success**: Rate as `ineffective` if the tutor completely spoon-feeds the steps to the point of mere compliance (the student just says "yeah" or computes isolated arithmetic without grasping the whole process). However, if the tutor's explanations, direct instruction, or step-by-step guidance successfully unblock the student and lead them to the correct answer with understanding, do not overly penalize the tutor for being directive. Rate productive guidance as `effective` or `partial`.
4. **Check student reception, not just tutor intent**: A tutor strategy is only as effective as the student's uptake. If the student gives minimal responses ("yeah," "okay," silence) throughout the moment, or actively signals disengagement, rate as `partial` or `ineffective` even if the tutor's technique was pedagogically sound in theory.
5. **Rapport that shows genuine caring is not "time displacement"**: Do not rate a rapport moment as `ineffective` solely because it takes time away from academics. If the tutor demonstrates authentic effort -- following through on a promise, accommodating a student's request, or persisting through difficulties to share something the student wants -- rate as `effective` or `partial`.
6. **Calibrate scaffolding to cumulative performance**: When a student has answered several problems correctly in a row, a single "why?" is not sufficient rigor -- the tutor should increase difficulty or remove supports. Conversely, when a student is clearly struggling or time is short, walking through steps or providing a worked example is acceptable scaffolding, not spoon-feeding.
