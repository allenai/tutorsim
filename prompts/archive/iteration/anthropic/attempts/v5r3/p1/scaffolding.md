You are an expert in pedagogy analyzing a K-12 math tutoring transcript.

## Research Context

We are studying how tutors in K-12 math tutoring conversations decide when to push for rigor versus when to introduce scaffolds. Your task is to identify turn ranges where a scaffolding/rigor key moment occurs — a moment where the tutor's choice about how much support to provide meaningfully shapes the student's learning.

### Scaffolding

Scaffolding is temporary, calibrated support that helps a student accomplish a task they cannot yet do independently. It is gradually removed as the student gains competence. The goal is to keep the student in their zone of proximal development — challenged but not overwhelmed.

Common scaffolding strategies:
- **Breaking down**: Decomposing a complex problem into smaller, manageable steps
- **Hinting**: Providing a cue that points the student toward the answer without giving it away
- **Modeling**: Demonstrating a process or worked example for the student to follow
- **Simplifying**: Reducing the complexity of the task while preserving the core concept
- **Visual/concrete aids**: Using diagrams, manipulatives, or real-world analogies

Over-scaffolding occurs when the tutor provides more support than the student needs — doing the cognitive work for them, giving away answers, or funneling toward a solution without letting the student struggle productively.

**Important**: The mere presence of scaffolding does NOT make a key moment. Scaffolding is what tutors DO — it is their primary tool. A key moment arises only when the scaffolding is notably miscalibrated (too much, too little, wrong type) OR when the student's signal creates a genuine fork in how to scaffold. If the tutor is simply teaching a procedure in a standard way and the student is following along, that is routine instruction, not a key moment.

### Rigor (Pushing for Depth)

Rigor means maintaining or increasing the cognitive demand on the student. It fosters independence and deeper understanding.

Common rigor strategies:
- **Requiring explanation**: "How did you know that?" or "Why does that work?"
- **Extending problems**: Adding complexity, edge cases, or transfer to new contexts
- **Fading support**: Deliberately removing scaffolds to see if the student can work independently
- **Challenging correct answers**: Asking students to generalize, prove, or find alternative approaches
- **Metacognition prompts**: Asking the student to reflect on their own thinking process

### The Core Tradeoff

At each moment, the tutor decides how much support to provide. Too much support robs the student of productive struggle. Too little support leaves the student floundering. The right choice depends on reading the student's current state — their understanding, confidence, and engagement.

## What Makes a Key Moment

Not every instance of teaching is a key moment. Most tutoring sessions contain stretches of routine instruction — introducing a topic, reading a problem, giving a standard explanation — that are unremarkable. A key moment is one where the tutor faces a **genuine fork** in how much cognitive support to provide, and a different tutor might reasonably make a different choice.

For a moment to qualify, all three of the following must be true:

1. **There is a signal — from the student or from the pattern of the session.** The student does something that creates a scaffolding/rigor decision point — makes an error, gives a correct but shallow answer, says "I don't know," shows a misconception, demonstrates unexpected insight, or otherwise reveals something about their understanding that the tutor must respond to. **Alternatively, the pattern of the session itself creates a signal**: the student has been answering too-easy questions correctly for many turns (suggesting the difficulty should increase), the student has been off-task or idle, or the session structure reveals a pedagogical gap (e.g., no math instruction has occurred, or the tutor chooses activities with no clear learning target). A tutor introducing a new topic or reading a problem aloud is not a key moment unless the student's response or the broader session context creates a fork.

2. **Multiple reasonable responses exist.** The tutor could go in meaningfully different directions — hint vs. tell, push for explanation vs. move on, simplify vs. let the student struggle, model the process vs. ask questions — and each path would shape the student's learning differently. If any competent tutor would do roughly the same thing, it is not a key moment. **Apply this criterion strictly**: when a tutor is simply teaching a new procedure step-by-step, or walking through a worked example, or explaining a concept for the first time — these are standard teaching moves that most tutors would do similarly. The moment becomes key only when the student's response (or non-response) creates a FORK that different tutors would handle differently.

3. **The choice has consequences for learning.** The tutor's decision at this point meaningfully affects whether the student does the cognitive work, develops understanding, or builds independence. A tutor saying "right, next problem" after a routine correct answer is low-stakes. A tutor saying "right, next problem" after a student got the answer but clearly doesn't understand why it works is high-stakes — the choice to push or move on shapes what the student actually learns.

**Common false positives to avoid:**
- Routine problem introduction or setup (reading the problem, defining terms)
- The tutor giving a standard explanation at a natural teaching point where any tutor would explain
- Correct answers to straightforward problems where there is nothing deeper to push on
- The tutor moving to the next problem in a natural sequence with no signal from the student
- **Most game-context interactions are NOT key moments.** In games (Blooket, speed multiplication, Broken Calculator, Fish Feeder, matching games, etc.): isolated mistakes, post-game score reviews, quick tips after games, and replaying games are all routine. Only flag a game-context moment if: (a) the tutor faces a significant decision about whether to stop the game to address a persistent misconception, (b) the game itself is clearly inappropriate for the student's level and the tutor's choice to use it is the key moment (flag as session-level), or (c) the student explicitly signals confusion or difficulty that the tutor must address. Simply playing games, even easy ones, is not a key moment unless the SESSION-LEVEL choice to play games is the issue.
- **Each sub-step of a multi-step problem that the tutor scaffolds** — if the tutor walks through an entire long division or word problem step by step, this is ONE moment (the decision to heavily scaffold), not five separate moments for each sub-step
- **Student self-corrections** that require no tutor intervention — if the student catches and fixes their own error, the tutor has no decision to make
- **Standard first-time instruction on a new procedure** — when a tutor introduces a new concept or procedure for the first time (e.g., explaining how to multiply fractions, demonstrating cross-cancellation, teaching long division), this is routine instruction unless the student signals confusion or the tutor's approach is notably miscalibrated. Teaching is not automatically a key moment; a key moment requires a **decision point** where the tutor's choice matters.
- **Post-game debrief or tips after correct performance** — if the student performed well in a game and the tutor offers a quick tip or reviews one question, this is routine unless the tip reveals a significant pedagogical choice (e.g., teaching a strategy the student didn't need)
- **Non-math activities with no pedagogical decision** — if the student plays a non-math game (e.g., pattern matching, butterfly matching) and the tutor passively observes, the game itself is not a key moment unless the tutor's CHOICE to allow it is the key moment (flag under session-level decisions instead)
- **Student working independently and correctly** — if the student solves a problem correctly on their own without struggle, and no scaffolding/rigor decision arises, this is not a key moment

## What to Look For

Scan the entire transcript for moments where scaffolding/rigor skill is tested:

- Student makes an error or shows a misconception, and the tutor must decide how to respond
- Student answers correctly but shallowly (bare answers, no explanation), and the tutor must decide whether to push — flag this when probing could reveal whether the student truly understands. Note: even for simpler questions, a **pattern** of 3+ consecutive correct answers with zero probing is a key moment because the tutor is consistently choosing pace over depth.
- **Repeated failure to probe understanding**: If the student answers multiple problems correctly in a row with only bare letter/number answers (e.g., "B", "six", "C") and the tutor never once asks "how did you get that?" or "why?", this cumulative failure IS a single key moment — the tutor is making a sustained choice not to check for understanding. Flag the full run of unprobed problems as one moment.
- Student says "I don't know" or is stuck, and the tutor must decide how much support to provide
- Student shows insight or makes a connection, and the tutor must decide whether to extend
- Tutor gives away the answer or does the cognitive work when the student could have done it
- Tutor explains when they could have questioned instead (or vice versa)
- Tutor repeats the same strategy that isn't working
- Tutor shifts approach mid-interaction (from questioning to telling, or from support to challenge)
- Student demonstrates independence or transfers a concept — the tutor must decide whether to fade support
- **Student completes a topic or quiz perfectly** (e.g., 5/5 correct) and says it was easy or reports being 'in the middle' — the tutor must decide whether to increase difficulty, probe deeper, or move to a new topic. If the tutor does NOT adjust, that is the key moment.
- **Student gives a correct answer that could mask a misconception** — the student might have guessed correctly, used a shortcut, or gotten the right answer for the wrong reason. If the tutor accepts without checking, that is a missed rigor opportunity.

### Session-Level Pedagogical Decisions (Flag These — They Are Often Missed)

Scan **proactively** for moments where the tutor makes (or fails to make) higher-level decisions that shape the learning opportunity. These are just as important as turn-level scaffolding decisions and are frequently the most consequential moments in a session:

- **Topic or activity selection**: Tutor chooses content that is clearly too easy or too hard for the student, or selects a game/activity without a clear learning goal. Example: student breezes through 5 problems correctly and the tutor starts the same difficulty level again.
- **Transition decisions**: Tutor moves between activities — was the transition purposeful or did it waste learning time? Example: tutor says 'I wonder what this game is' without stating a learning objective.
- **Break timing**: Tutor offers breaks before any work is done, or structures the session in a way that minimizes learning time.
- **Tutor disengagement or absence**: Tutor is absent, passive, or not redirecting a student who is off-task. If no math instruction occurs in a session, that IS a key moment.
- **Pacing choices**: Tutor spends disproportionate time on games, chat, or non-learning activities when the student needs instruction.
- **Difficulty not adjusted after demonstrated mastery**: Student gets 5/5 or answers multiple problems instantly, and tutor does not increase difficulty or change topic. The decision to continue with too-easy content IS a key moment.

**Session-level moments are typically short (1-5 turns)** — flag the specific decision point (e.g., the turn where the tutor selects the next activity, or the turn where the student finishes a set of easy problems and the tutor doesn't adjust).

These count as key moments when the tutor's choice (or inaction) meaningfully impacts how much learning occurs in the session.
- **Tutor moves on without probing** after a correct but potentially shallow answer, missing a chance to push for explanation or deeper understanding
- **Tutor fails to adjust difficulty** when the student breezes through multiple problems too easily — the content is below the student's level and the tutor doesn't increase challenge
- **Tutor provides unrequested help** after the student answered correctly, offering a 'tip' or strategy the student didn't need
- **Tutor does not act at all** at a moment where any attentive tutor would intervene — the absence of scaffolding or rigor-pushing IS the key moment

## How to Define Moment Boundaries

Draw turn_start and turn_end around the **full scaffolding arc**: from the turn where the scaffolding/rigor decision point first arises (e.g., the problem is introduced, or the student's first signal appears) through to the turn where the tutor's approach has fully played out and the problem or topic shifts.

**Boundary sizing guidance:**
- A moment can be short (3-5 turns) for a quick exchange, or long (20-80 turns) for an extended multi-step problem where the tutor's scaffolding approach persists.
- **Do not under-detect short moments.** A 3-turn exchange where the tutor gives away an answer, provides an unrequested hint, or asks 'are you sure?' that leads to self-correction is absolutely a valid key moment. Short does not mean unimportant.
- **Most moments should be 5-50 turns.** If your detection exceeds 60 turns, critically re-examine whether it contains multiple distinct decision points that should be separate detections.
- If the tutor walks a student through a single multi-step problem (e.g., one long division problem, one word problem), the whole problem-solving sequence is typically ONE moment — do not fragment it into sub-steps.
- However, if the tutor scaffolds MULTIPLE SEPARATE PROBLEMS in sequence (e.g., problem A, then problem B, then problem C), each problem with its own scaffolding arc should be a SEPARATE detection, even if the tutor uses the same approach throughout.
- Include the problem introduction/setup turns if the tutor's scaffolding approach begins there.
- End the moment when the activity clearly shifts to a new problem or topic.
- When in doubt about boundaries, **focus on the specific decision point** rather than expanding to capture context. A tighter detection that matches the critical exchange is better than a sprawling one that captures the whole lesson.

## How to Choose a Cut Point

Each detection needs a `suggested_cut_turn`. The transcript will be sliced at this turn, and a **different AI tutor** will take over from that point. They see everything up to and including the cut turn, and nothing after. Their first response is what gets evaluated.

A good cut point satisfies three criteria:

1. **Enough context to understand the situation.** The math topic, current problem, and the student's cognitive state should be clear from preceding turns. Relevant patterns (e.g., the student has been struggling for several turns, or coasting with bare answers) should be visible.

2. **A genuine decision to make.** The moment presents a fork where multiple reasonable scaffolding or rigor approaches exist, and the choice reveals something about tutor skill.

3. **No preview of the original tutor's approach.** The cut lands *before* the original tutor responds to the scaffolding-relevant moment, so the incoming tutor must make their own choice without being anchored.

The cut turn must be a **STUDENT turn** (or non-tutor turn) so the tutor speaks next. Place the cut at the student turn that sets up the decision.

## Detection Quantity Guidance

A typical 30-60 minute tutoring session contains **3-10 key moments**. If you find yourself detecting more than 12 moments in a single session, re-examine whether some are routine instruction rather than genuine decision points. If you find fewer than 2, re-scan for missed rigor opportunities and session-level decisions.

Similarly, be wary of any single detection spanning more than 80 turns. While rare exceptions exist (e.g., an entire session with no math instruction), most key moments are focused decision arcs of 5-50 turns.

## Consolidation Rule

Before finalizing your output, review all your detections and **merge overlapping moments only if they address the exact same problem and decision**. If the tutor scaffolds a student through a single problem across many turns, that should be ONE detection. However:
- Two detections addressing **different problems** should remain separate even if they are adjacent in the transcript.
- Two detections addressing **different types of decisions** (e.g., one is about scaffolding an error, another is about failing to push rigor on a correct answer) should remain separate even if they overlap in turns.
- If merging would create a detection longer than 80 turns, reconsider whether the moments are truly the same decision.
- A session-level moment (e.g., pacing, activity choice) should be separate from a content-level moment (e.g., scaffolding an error) even if they overlap in time.
