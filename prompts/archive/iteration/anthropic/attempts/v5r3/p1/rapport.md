You are an expert in pedagogy analyzing a K-12 math tutoring transcript.

## Research Context

We are studying rapport building behaviors in K-12 math tutoring conversations. Your task is to identify turn ranges where a rapport-relevant key moment occurs — a moment where the tutor's rapport choice meaningfully shapes the student relationship or learning environment.

### What Is Rapport?

Rapport is the interpersonal connection between tutor and student that creates a safe, productive learning environment. In tutoring, rapport serves learning — it is the foundation that makes academic work possible, not an end in itself.

Rapport includes:
- **Trust and safety**: The student feels comfortable making mistakes, asking questions, and expressing confusion
- **Genuine interest**: The tutor shows authentic curiosity about the student as a person, not just a learner
- **Emotional attunement**: The tutor notices and responds to the student's emotional state (frustration, excitement, fatigue, anxiety)
- **Relational continuity**: The tutor remembers details from prior sessions, follows up on personal sharing, and builds a relationship over time

### Rapport Strategies

Common rapport-building approaches tutors use:
- **Check-ins**: Asking about the student's day, feelings, or life outside tutoring
- **Personal sharing**: The tutor shares something about themselves to build reciprocity
- **Specific praise**: Acknowledging particular effort or growth ("You stuck with that even when it was hard")
- **Humor and playfulness**: Lightening the mood, making math fun
- **Emotional validation**: Acknowledging frustration or difficulty ("I get it, this is a tough one")
- **Interest integration**: Using the student's interests to frame problems or examples
- **Structured choices**: Giving the student agency ("Want to try angles or fractions next?")

### Rapport and Learning

The challenge of rapport in tutoring is calibrating it to the student and the moment. A tutor who spends 10 minutes on social conversation builds connection but at the cost of instructional time. A tutor who ignores a student's frustration preserves time but loses engagement. A tutor who gives generic praise ("Good job!") fills the rapport slot without building genuine connection.

## What Makes a Key Moment

Not every social exchange is a key moment. Most tutoring sessions contain dozens of small rapport-adjacent interactions — greetings, brief praise, routine transitions — that are unremarkable. A key moment is one where the tutor's rapport skill is revealed — either through how they respond to a rapport-relevant situation OR through their failure to respond when the situation called for it. A different tutor with different rapport skills might handle the same moment in a meaningfully different way, whether that means engaging more deeply, more briefly, more personally, or simply noticing something the original tutor missed.

For a moment to qualify, all three of the following must be true:

1. **There is a rapport-relevant decision point.** Usually this comes from a student signal — the student expresses frustration, shares something personal, disengages, resists, shows excitement, or otherwise changes the emotional or relational texture of the session. However, decision points also arise from **session structure**: openings and closings inherently demand rapport choices (depth of check-in, whether to follow up, how to reflect on learning, how to preview the next session). A tutor who rushes through an opening or closing has made a rapport choice just as much as one who lingers. Similarly, transitions from social conversation to academic work, or moments where the tutor decides whether to personalize content, create decision points even without a dramatic student signal.

2. **Multiple reasonable responses exist.** The tutor could go in meaningfully different directions — validate vs. redirect, engage personally vs. stay on task, address emotion vs. ignore it — and each path would shape the relationship or learning environment differently. If any competent tutor would do roughly the same thing, it is not a key moment.

3. **The choice has consequences.** The tutor's decision at this point meaningfully affects trust, safety, engagement, or the student's willingness to take intellectual risks. A "Good job" after a correct answer is low-stakes. A "Good job" after a student struggled through something they wanted to give up on is high-stakes — what the tutor says here shapes whether the student persists next time.

### Missed Opportunities Are Key Moments

A key moment does NOT require the tutor to do something noteworthy. If the session context or student behavior creates an opportunity for rapport and the tutor **ignores it, skips it, or handles it superficially**, that IS a key moment — it reveals the tutor's rapport skill (or lack thereof) just as clearly as an active, engaged response would. Human experts specifically annotate these missed opportunities. Common examples include:

- **Shallow check-ins**: Tutor asks "How are you?" but doesn't follow up when the student gives a one-word answer, missing a chance to build genuine connection
- **Abrupt closings**: Tutor ends with "Good job, bye" without reflecting on what was learned, previewing the next session, or making a personal connection
- **Ignored student excitement**: Student celebrates getting an answer right ("Yay!", "My math is now mathing!") and the tutor moves to the next problem without acknowledgment
- **Glossed-over personal sharing**: Student mentions something personal (upcoming surgery, family situation, hobby) and the tutor responds minimally or redirects to academics without engaging
- **Unaddressed struggle**: Student shows confusion or frustration and the tutor addresses only the academic content without checking in emotionally
- **Active rapport damage**: The tutor displays behaviors that ERODE trust or safety — speaking with exasperation or condescension ('Are you guessing or actually reading?'), criticizing student effort ('That's pretty low effort'), using a flat or impatient tone during student struggle, calling the student 'smart' in ways that create fixed-mindset pressure, or repeatedly interrupting the student's problem-solving to provide their own approach. These moments are especially important to flag because they actively harm the learning relationship.

When annotating a missed opportunity, include the turns where the opportunity arose AND the tutor's insufficient response (or lack of response). The key moment is the gap between what happened and what could have happened.

### Calibrating Detection Threshold

Not every student emotion or personal comment constitutes a key moment. To qualify, the interaction should have **enough substance** that different tutors would handle it meaningfully differently. Use these filters:

- **Sustained interaction test**: Does the rapport-relevant exchange span multiple turns of genuine back-and-forth, or is it a single student comment that the tutor briefly acknowledges? A student saying "yay!" and the tutor saying "nice" before moving on is generally NOT a key moment. A student saying "yay!" and the tutor either celebrating at length OR conspicuously ignoring it IS a key moment.
  - NOT key moments: Student shares a fun fact in passing and tutor says 'cool' (1-2 turns); student compliments tutor briefly and tutor says 'thank you' (1-2 turns); student uses a Spanish word and tutor engages for 3-4 turns before moving on; student mentions their username origin and tutor says 'all right, cool' without follow-up; student expresses brief curiosity about a historical figure and moves on.
  - ARE key moments: Student shares personal information AND tutor either engages meaningfully (5+ turns of follow-up) OR conspicuously fails to engage despite the significance of the sharing; student celebrates a breakthrough after sustained struggle and tutor's response reveals whether they recognize the emotional significance.
- **Consequence test**: Would the tutor's handling of this specific moment plausibly affect the student's trust, engagement, or willingness to be vulnerable? A student sharing their username origin in passing has lower stakes than a student disclosing a family situation.
- **Novelty test**: Does this moment introduce new emotional or relational information, or is it a continuation of the session's baseline tone? A student who has been cheerful all session saying one more cheerful thing is not a new decision point.

When in doubt about a mid-session moment, ask: "If I showed this 5-turn excerpt to a teaching coach, would they have specific feedback about how the tutor handled this?" If the answer is yes, include it. If the answer is "the tutor did the only reasonable thing," skip it.

**Common false positives to avoid:**
- Brief generic praise after a single correct answer with no preceding struggle or emotional context ("Good job" after an easy, routine problem)
- Routine transitions between individual problems where the tutor is simply moving to the next question without any rapport-relevant exchange
- A single polite or warm comment in passing that does not constitute a sustained interaction or respond to a student signal
- A brief off-topic student comment that the tutor acknowledges in one turn and moves on — no sustained exchange develops
- A student celebration ('Yay!', 'My math is now mathing!') that the tutor briefly acknowledges ('Nice', 'All right') without either sustained celebration or conspicuous dismissal — the moment is pleasant but unremarkable
- A student sharing a personal detail in passing (nickname origin, favorite food, brief curiosity about a topic) where the tutor acknowledges it in 1-2 turns and neither party develops the thread further
- A brief playful or cultural exchange (e.g., student uses a foreign word, tutor responds) that lasts 3-5 turns but doesn't connect to the student's identity, interests, or emotional state in a meaningful way

**Important: Session openings and closings should almost always be flagged.** A brief, formulaic opening ("How are you?" / "Good" / move to math) is typically a key moment because it reveals the tutor's rapport skill — a different tutor might ask follow-up questions, do a check-in activity, or build personal connection. Similarly, a rushed or generic closing ("Good job, bye") is typically a key moment. Always flag openings and closings UNLESS the session boundary consists of nothing more than a single "Hi"/"Bye" exchange with zero opportunity for rapport (e.g., a one-word greeting immediately followed by content with no pause, or a student who leaves abruptly with no tutor present to respond). If there is ANY check-in attempt, any preview, any praise, or any conversational exchange at the boundary — flag it.

## What to Look For

Scan the entire transcript for moments where rapport skill is tested:

- Student expresses emotion (frustration, excitement, tiredness, boredom, anxiety) and the tutor must decide how to respond
- **Student academic vulnerability**: The student takes an interpersonal risk by admitting they don't know something ('I need some help,' 'I forgot,' 'I don't really know'), expressing fear of failure ('But what if I get it wrong again?'), or signaling they can't follow along ('Hello?' when tutor has been monologuing). The tutor's response shapes whether the student will be vulnerable again. A tutor who responds warmly ('That's totally fine, let's work through it together') builds safety. A tutor who responds dismissively or doesn't acknowledge the admission at all erodes it. Even brief exchanges (2-3 turns) can qualify if the student's vulnerability is genuine.
- Student disengagement builds over multiple turns (shorter responses, "idk", listlessness)
- **Student physically present but emotionally absent**: Student is eating, distracted by surroundings, doing other things while tutoring. The tutor must decide how to acknowledge this, adapt the session, or re-engage the student. Note especially when the student's off-task behavior (eating, showing objects, talking to family members) becomes the dominant dynamic of the session. When a student is eating throughout a session, each distinct food-related tangent (e.g., student shows their pizza, student comments on burnt fries, student discusses their drink) may constitute a separate rapport moment because the tutor repeatedly faces the decision of whether to engage with the distraction or redirect to academics. Do not merge all food exchanges into one detection if they occur at different points in the session with academic work in between.
- Student shares personal information and the tutor must decide whether/how to engage
- Student resistance or refusal where the tutor must navigate tension
- Tutor attempts humor, playfulness, or personal connection — especially when it falls flat or creates awkwardness
- Transitions between activities where the tutor has a real choice (offering agency vs. directing)
- **Session structure choices that build trust**: The tutor's decisions about session pacing and structure reveal rapport skill. Key examples include: offering a break and holding to the time limit (builds accountability and trust); reviewing prior session material before new content (builds confidence); previewing what comes next so the student can mentally prepare; ending a session early as an act of generosity or care. These are 'invisible' rapport strategies — they build safety through structure rather than explicit emotional engagement.
- Session openings or closings that go beyond formulaic exchange (meaningful check-in, reflective wrap-up)
- Moments where the tutor misses a rapport opportunity — the student signals something the tutor ignores
- **Academic content personalized for rapport**: The tutor incorporates the student's interests, preferences, or life into math examples (e.g., using the student's favorite candy in a division problem, framing problems around the student's hobbies). This is rapport THROUGH academics — the tutor's choice to personalize reveals relational investment. Also note when the tutor misses an opportunity to personalize (using generic examples when they know the student's interests).
- **Scaffolding with emotional attunement**: During academic work, the tutor's approach reveals rapport skill — not only during sustained struggle but also in brief moments. Key decision points include:
  - **Asking vs. telling**: Does the tutor ask for the student's thinking before explaining, or do they immediately provide the procedure? A tutor who says 'How did you get that?' before correcting builds more trust than one who says 'No, here's how you do it.'
  - **Patience with wrong answers**: When a student gives a wrong answer, does the tutor respond with curiosity ('Interesting, walk me through your thinking') or with dismissal ('Try again')? Even brief exchanges (2-3 turns) around wrong answers are key moments if the tutor's response reveals their patience level.
  - **Building on student ideas vs. overriding**: When a student offers partial or flawed reasoning, does the tutor build on what's correct or start from scratch with their own approach?
  - **Over-scaffolding**: A tutor who gives away the entire procedure as a 'hint' or who solves the problem for the student reveals low expectations and undermines the student's sense of competence.
  - **Tutor admitting confusion or errors**: How the tutor handles their own mistakes (misreading a problem, getting confused) affects whether the student feels safe making mistakes too.
  - **Checking understanding genuinely**: A tutor who asks 'Does that make sense?' and waits for a real answer vs. one who asks and immediately moves on.
- **Student agency and choice**: The tutor offers the student meaningful choices about what to work on, how to approach a problem, or how to structure the session. Note especially when a student requests something ("Can we do art?", "Can we do spelling?") and the tutor must decide whether to honor or redirect.
- Power dynamics where the tutor shows flexibility or rigidity about rules, pacing, or time

## How to Define Moment Boundaries

Draw turn_start and turn_end around the **full rapport interaction**: from the turn that initiates the rapport-relevant moment to the turn where the conversation clearly shifts to a different mode (e.g., social conversation ends and academic work begins, or vice versa). Do NOT clip to just the triggering moment — include the entire exchange that constitutes the rapport event.

**Sizing guidance:**
- **Session openings**: Start from the first greeting turn through the point where academic work clearly begins. If the tutor spends 2 turns on "How are you?" and moves to math, the detection is 2-5 turns. If the tutor spends 50 turns on personal conversation, games, or check-in activities, the detection is 50 turns. Either way, it is ONE detection.
- **Session closings**: Start from where the tutor begins wrapping up (e.g., "That's all for today") through the final goodbye.
- **Extended social conversations**: If the tutor and student have a sustained conversation on a SINGLE rapport theme (e.g., discussing a hobby, talking about a family trip), include the entire exchange as one detection. However, if the conversation shifts to a clearly DIFFERENT rapport theme — e.g., from talking about school to disclosing an upcoming surgery, or from a check-in game to discussing food preferences — create SEPARATE detections for each thematically distinct segment. The test is: could a teaching coach give different feedback on each segment? If yes, they are separate moments.
- **Session openings with multiple phases**: If a session opening contains distinct rapport phases (e.g., a brief check-in, then a get-to-know-you game, then a personal disclosure), split them into separate detections when each phase has a different rapport character. A formulaic 'How are you?' exchange is one detection; a subsequent two-truths-and-a-lie game is another; a student's disclosure about surgery is yet another.
- **Mid-session rapport moments**: Include the triggering student behavior, the tutor's full response, and any follow-up exchange until the conversation returns to the previous activity.

Never create multiple overlapping or adjacent detections for what is essentially one continuous rapport interaction.

## How to Choose a Cut Point

Each detection needs a `suggested_cut_turn`. The transcript will be sliced at this turn, and a **different AI tutor** will take over from that point. They see everything up to and including the cut turn, and nothing after. Their first response is what gets evaluated.

A good cut point satisfies three criteria:

1. **Enough context to understand the situation.** The student's emotional and behavioral state should be readable from preceding turns. Relevant patterns (e.g., disengagement building over several turns, or a sudden emotional shift) should be visible in the transcript before the cut.

2. **A genuine decision to make.** The moment presents a fork where multiple reasonable tutor responses exist, and the choice reveals something about tutor skill.

3. **No preview of the original tutor's approach.** The cut lands *before* the original tutor responds to the rapport-relevant moment, so the incoming tutor must make their own choice without being anchored.

The cut turn must be a **STUDENT turn** (or non-tutor turn) so the tutor speaks next. Place the cut at the student turn that sets up the decision.
