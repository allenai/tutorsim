You are an expert in pedagogy analyzing a K-12 tutoring transcript. Your task is to identify ALL turn ranges where rapport-related pedagogical events occur.

Choose the tightest turn range that still contains the full relational event. Start where the rapport move actually begins, and end once the interaction returns to ordinary instruction/procedure. Do not include long lead-ins or trailing explanation unless they are part of the rapport moment itself.

If one social event naturally unfolds across many turns, return one span. If there are two distinct rapport events close together (e.g., a check-in, then later a choice-giving transition), return separate spans.

## What to Look For

Scan the ENTIRE transcript for ANY moment involving social-psychological dynamics:

- Session opening / greeting / check-in ONLY when it contains more than a bare greeting or procedural start (e.g., personal check-in, follow-up, warmth, choice, humor, emotional attunement, or meaningful small talk)
- Session closing / goodbye / wrap-up ONLY when it contains more than a routine sign-off (e.g., praise, reflection, future planning, warmth, student choice, or emotional acknowledgment)
- Transitions between activities ONLY when the transition has relational significance (e.g., offering choice, easing resistance, inviting readiness, validating feelings, or connecting to student interests)
- Student expresses emotion (frustration, excitement, tiredness, boredom, anxiety)
- Tutor acknowledges OR fails to acknowledge student emotional state, confusion, frustration, distraction, or communication difficulty
- Small talk, personal sharing, off-topic conversation, or student-led tangents that temporarily shift away from the lesson
- Praise or affirmation (generic or specific), including very short moments such as "good job," "perfect," "you did really well," or warm acknowledgment after a correct answer
- Humor, playfulness, teasing, joking, or light banter, including moments where the tutor follows the student's tangent and then gently returns to the task
- Student resistance, refusal, disengagement, off-task behavior, or signs of not hearing/not following
- Tutor builds on student interests or remembers personal details
- Power dynamics (tutor flexibility or rigidity about rules, posture, time)
- Student shares personal information unprompted
- Moments where rapport should have been supported but the tutor responds flatly, rigidly, dismissively, or misses the opportunity
- Academic content used as rapport: making problems personal, using student interests, connecting concepts to the student's life, offering motivating formats/rewards, doing a confidence-building review, giving the student meaningful choice/control, or adjusting the task to meet the student's comfort level

## Important: Rapport Can Be Embedded Inside Teaching

Do not limit rapport to greetings, praise, or small talk. Also detect instructional moves that serve a relational function, such as:
- checking what the student already knows before introducing new content
- reviewing prior material to create comfort or confidence
- offering the student choice about what to work on or how to work
- adapting an example to the student's interests/preferences
- using drawing, games, or other motivating formats to support engagement
- gently scaffolding after an error in a way that protects the student's confidence

## Important: Be Comprehensive but Selective

Identify all clearly notable rapport moments, but do NOT force a quota. Most sessions will contain roughly **3-8** rapport moments, though some may have fewer or more.

Prefer moments that a human annotator would judge as a distinct relational event. Do **not** include routine procedural talk, bare acknowledgments, or generic instruction unless they carry clear relational significance.

When in doubt, ask: "Is there a meaningful social, emotional, motivational, or relational move here, beyond ordinary tutoring flow?" If not, do not include it.

## Do NOT Include
- bare greetings like "hi" / "hello" with no meaningful follow-up
- routine screen-sharing, loading, or platform navigation
- generic directions, comprehension checks, or procedural transitions by themselves
- every instance of "right," "okay," "good," or "perfect" unless it functions as a distinct relational move
