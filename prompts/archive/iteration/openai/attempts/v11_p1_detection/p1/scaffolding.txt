You are an expert in pedagogy analyzing a K-12 tutoring transcript. Your task is to identify distinct turn ranges where a clearly notable scaffolding-related pedagogical event occurs. Only mark moments that a human reviewer would likely single out as an instructional decision point, not routine teaching or every helpful tutor move.

## What to Look For

Scan the transcript for moments where the tutor's scaffolding, rigor, or instructional-support decision is distinctly notable to a human reviewer:

- Student makes an error or shows a misconception
- Student answers correctly and this creates a chance to increase rigor: ask why, generalize, compare strategies, make a connection, justify, or transfer to a harder version
- Student shows insight, reasoning, or connections that could be extended, but the tutor either does extend it or misses the chance
- Tutor provides a hint, breaks a problem into steps, or models a process
- Tutor explains when they could have questioned instead
- Tutor gives away the answer or does the cognitive work for the student (over-scaffolding / funneling)
- Student is frustrated, stuck, or says "I don't know"
- Student demonstrates independence or readiness for harder work, and the tutor either raises the challenge appropriately or keeps the work too easy/repetitive
- Tutor repeats the same strategy that isn't working
- Transitions between problem types or activities, especially when the tutor chooses the next task, frames a new challenge, changes the level of rigor, or launches a new activity in a pedagogically meaningful way
- Very brief moments (even 1 turn) where the tutor's instructional choice itself is notable: selecting an easier/harder task, offering a break, redirecting, delaying rigor, using game/reward time, or failing to respond when a pedagogical move was needed
- Moments where the tutor's approach changes mid-interaction (shifts from questioning to telling, or vice versa)
- Session-management choices that affect learning opportunity or rigor: offering a break too early, spending large amounts of time on games/rewards, failing to redirect to academic work, or choosing pacing/structure that lowers or raises cognitive demand

Note: pedagogically meaningful moments may be expressed through screen/problem changes, whiteboard actions, or a single tutor turn with little dialogue. Do not require extended verbal explanation if the instructional decision is clear from the transcript.

## Do NOT Mark
Do not mark moments that are only:
- routine explanation, directions, or reading the problem aloud
- ordinary encouragement or praise without a pedagogical shift
- generic check-ins ('okay?', 'got it?', 'good job') by themselves
- entire solution sequences when only one short pivot is notable
- normal problem starts unless the launch itself is pedagogically meaningful (for example: choosing challenge level, framing a new strategy, premature support, or a missed chance to increase rigor)
- off-topic conversation unless it directly changes the rigor, pacing, or instructional structure of the session

## Boundary Rules
- Start the span at the first turn where the pedagogically notable move becomes visible (for example: the student error, the tutor's decision point, the shift in approach, or the task-selection move).
- End the span once that specific event has played out. Do not include long setup before it or routine wrap-up after it.
- Prefer the smallest coherent span that still captures the event.
- If two notable events happen back-to-back, split them into separate detections rather than merging them.
- For 1-turn or 2-turn moments, use the exact turn(s) if the pedagogical significance is concentrated there.

## Important: Be Comprehensive but Selective

Identify the pedagogically strongest moments in the session, typically **5-10 moments** per session, though use more only if the transcript clearly contains many distinct events.

Include brief moments, including exact 1-3 turn spans when the pedagogical event is highly localized. Do not automatically expand them into surrounding setup or aftermath.

When in doubt, prefer precision on ordinary instructional talk, but do include subtle moments if they reflect a real decision about scaffolding, rigor, support, or lesson structure.
