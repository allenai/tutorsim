### Scaffolding

Scaffolding is temporary, calibrated support that helps a student accomplish a task they cannot yet do independently. It is gradually removed as the student gains competence. The goal is to keep the student in their zone of proximal development -- challenged but not overwhelmed.

Common scaffolding strategies:
- **Breaking down**: Decomposing a complex problem into smaller, manageable steps
- **Hinting**: Providing a cue that points the student toward the answer without giving it away
- **Modeling**: Demonstrating a process or worked example for the student to follow
- **Simplifying**: Reducing the complexity of the task while preserving the core concept
- **Visual/concrete aids**: Using diagrams, manipulatives, or real-world analogies

Over-scaffolding occurs when the tutor provides more support than the student needs -- doing the cognitive work for them, giving away answers, or not giving the student a chance to try. However, the threshold for what counts as "too much" depends heavily on context:

- When a topic is **new to the student**, heavier scaffolding (modeling, worked examples, step-by-step guidance) is expected and appropriate. The first problem on a new concept should involve significant tutor support.
- When a student is **confused, stuck, or frustrated**, more scaffolding is warranted. A tutor who explains and guides a struggling student is doing their job, not over-scaffolding.
- Over-scaffolding IS a concern when:
  - The student has **already done similar problems** with tutor help and the tutor continues the same level of scaffolding without fading support. After 2-3 guided problems, the student should be given a chance to try independently.
  - The tutor **scaffolds before checking** what the student can do — jumping in with hints, structure, or step-by-step guidance before the student has attempted the problem.
  - The tutor **does physical or computational work** the student could do themselves — such as writing on the board, computing intermediate steps, or narrating the procedure while the student watches.
  - The student **answers every sub-question correctly and quickly** but the tutor continues to break down subsequent problems at the same granularity.

### Rigor (Pushing for Depth)

Rigor means maintaining or increasing the cognitive demand on the student. It fosters independence and deeper understanding.

Common rigor strategies:
- **Requiring explanation**: "How did you know that?" or "Why does that work?"
- **Extending problems**: Adding complexity, edge cases, or transfer to new contexts
- **Fading support**: Deliberately removing scaffolds to see if the student can work independently
- **Challenging correct answers**: Asking students to generalize, prove, or find alternative approaches
- **Metacognition prompts**: Asking the student to reflect on their own thinking process

### The Core Tradeoff

At each moment, the tutor decides how much support to provide. Too much support robs the student of productive struggle. Too little support leaves the student floundering. The right choice depends on reading the student's current state -- their understanding, confidence, and engagement.

### What Counts as Evidence of Student Progress

When assessing results, consider the following as valid evidence of progress:
- The student produces a correct answer through their own reasoning (not guessing)
- The student self-corrects an error after a tutor prompt
- The student applies a procedure or concept they couldn't before
- The student articulates an idea in their own words
- The student correctly answers sub-questions that require thought
- The student shows increased confidence or engagement with the material

Do NOT require the student to demonstrate deep conceptual understanding or transfer to count a strategy as effective. For procedural skills especially, correctly executing the procedure with active participation IS progress. Similarly, a strategy that sets up future learning (e.g., introducing a concept that the student will practice next) can be effective even if the immediate evidence is modest.

Conversely, the following are NOT sufficient evidence of progress:
- The student says "mhm," "yeah," or "okay" without demonstrating understanding
- The student selects the correct multiple-choice answer after the tutor has narrated the reasoning
- The student repeats back what the tutor said
- The student guesses and happens to be correct

## Calibrating Your Result Assessment

Your result assessment should reflect the **overall effectiveness** of the tutor's strategy, not a checklist of flaws.

- **Effective**: The tutor's strategy helped the student make progress. The student engaged in thinking, corrected a misconception, applied a concept, produced correct answers through their own reasoning, or demonstrated understanding they didn't have before. A strategy is effective if the student did meaningful cognitive work and arrived at a correct or improved understanding — full stop. Do NOT downgrade to partial because:
  - The tutor could have pushed for more independence
  - The tutor could have asked an additional follow-up question
  - The tutor scaffolded more than the strict minimum needed
  - The student used a less elegant method that still worked
  - You can imagine a better alternative approach
  A strategy does not need to be optimal to be effective. If a reasonable approach produced a reasonable outcome where the student did real thinking, it was effective.

- **Partially effective**: The strategy produced some progress but had a **specific, identifiable** flaw that clearly and demonstrably limited the student's learning. Partial is a narrow category — it is NOT the default when you see both positives and negatives. To rate partial, you must be able to point to a concrete moment where something went wrong that materially reduced learning. Examples that warrant partial: the student corrected an error but strong evidence shows they memorized a rule without understanding; the tutor addressed the wrong misconception; the tutor's explanation actively confused the student before eventually helping; the student learned part of the concept but a tutor error prevented them from grasping the full idea. If you are unsure between effective and partial, ask: did the student do meaningful cognitive work and make progress? If yes, rate effective. If you are unsure between partial and ineffective, ask: is there concrete evidence the student learned something they didn't know before? If not, rate ineffective.

- **Ineffective**: The strategy failed to produce student learning or missed a clear learning opportunity. Rate as ineffective when ANY of these apply:
  - The student remained stuck, disengaged, or guessed without reasoning
  - The student only gave compliant responses ("mhm," "yeah," "okay") without demonstrating understanding
  - The tutor did the cognitive work for the student — computing, writing, narrating steps — while the student passively watched or confirmed
  - The student was coasting (answering easily and correctly) and the tutor did not increase challenge
  - A clear opportunity to scaffold or push was available but the tutor let the moment pass
  - The tutor's approach was theoretically sound but the student did not actually benefit from it
  Do NOT upgrade to partial just because the tutor chose a reasonable approach, used a good analogy, or had good intentions. A well-chosen strategy that fails to produce student learning is still ineffective. What matters is what the student actually gained.

When writing your result, **commit to an overall assessment first** — did this work or didn't it? Then support that assessment with evidence. Do not write a balanced pros-and-cons analysis; write an argument for your conclusion. If the strategy was effective, say so clearly and do not undercut it with 'however' clauses about improvements. If the strategy was ineffective, say so clearly and do not soften it with praise for the tutor's intentions. Only note a weakness if it materially changed the outcome; only note a strength if it materially contributed to learning.
