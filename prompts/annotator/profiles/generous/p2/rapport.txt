### What Is Rapport?

Rapport is the interpersonal connection between tutor and student that creates a safe, productive learning environment. In tutoring, rapport serves learning -- it is the foundation that makes academic work possible, not an end in itself.

Rapport includes:
- **Trust and safety**: The student feels comfortable making mistakes, asking questions, and expressing confusion
- **Genuine interest**: The tutor shows authentic curiosity about the student as a person, not just a learner
- **Emotional attunement**: The tutor notices and responds to the student's emotional state (frustration, excitement, fatigue, anxiety)
- **Relational continuity**: The tutor remembers details from prior sessions, follows up on personal sharing, and builds a relationship over time

### Rapport Strategies

Common rapport-building approaches tutors use:
- **Check-ins**: Asking about the student's day, feelings, or life outside tutoring
- **Personal sharing**: The tutor shares something about themselves to build reciprocity
- **Specific praise**: Acknowledging particular effort or growth ("You stuck with that even when it was hard")
- **Humor and playfulness**: Lightening the mood, making math fun
- **Emotional validation**: Acknowledging frustration or difficulty ("I get it, this is a tough one")
- **Interest integration**: Using the student's interests to frame problems or examples
- **Structured choices**: Giving the student agency ("Want to try angles or fractions next?")
- **Session management as rapport**: Offering breaks, previewing upcoming work, managing smooth transitions, setting clear expectations -- these professional practices build trust and safety even without explicit rapport language
- **Patience and steady presence**: Staying calm through repeated errors, not showing frustration, giving the student time to think -- this communicates that the tutor is a safe person to struggle in front of
- **Accountability and follow-through**: Keeping to time commitments, remembering where the session left off, delivering on promises -- these build reliability-based trust

### Rapport and Learning

The challenge of rapport in tutoring is calibrating it to the student and the moment. A tutor who spends 10 minutes on social conversation builds connection but at the cost of instructional time. A tutor who ignores a student's frustration preserves time but loses engagement. A tutor who exclusively relies on generic praise ('Good job!') session after session, without ever adapting to specific student actions, may plateau in building connection. However, brief generic praise or standard check-ins can be perfectly appropriate in context -- especially during fast-paced problem work, with reserved students, or when the tutor adapts after a minimal response.

### Calibrating Your Assessment

When writing the result, calibrate your assessment against these standards:

- **Effective** does not require extraordinary or creative rapport. A standard check-in that is warm, well-timed, and transitions smoothly IS effective. A professional session closing with a preview of next session IS effective. The bar is: was the tutor's approach reasonable, contextually appropriate, and did it visibly serve the student-tutor relationship? Effective moments show some evidence of connection beyond a purely formulaic exchange — the tutor adapted to the student's response, the student engaged in return, or the tutor did something substantive (reduced pressure, offered agency, normalized struggle) rather than just saying 'Good' or 'Perfect.' A single brief affirmation ('Perfect') after a student confirmation ('Yeah') is normal instruction, not necessarily a rapport success.
- **Partial** means the tutor made a recognizable attempt but the result was ambiguous or fell clearly short. Examples: a check-in with zero follow-up when the student signaled distress; praise so disconnected it felt random; a rapport attempt that consumed excessive instructional time without payoff; or a reasonable approach where the impact on the relationship is genuinely indiscernible — the student gave no visible reaction, the exchange was purely formulaic, or it is simply too early to tell whether the approach landed. When you honestly cannot determine whether the tutor's approach built rapport, partial is the appropriate rating.
- **Ineffective** means the tutor missed a clear opportunity with visible negative consequences -- e.g., ignoring a student's emotional bid, creating discomfort, being entirely absent when connection was needed, using a dismissive or exasperated tone, or providing flat/minimal responses through a stretch where the student clearly needed support. Visible negative consequences include: the student becoming defensive or withdrawn, repeated disengagement or random guessing, the student seeking help from someone else (e.g., a parent), or the student's emotional state visibly worsening. When the overall relational impact of the tutor's behavior during the detected moment was negative, the presence of one minor positive element (e.g., a single structured choice or a brief 'you're good') does not elevate the result to partial.

Important: Do not downgrade a result just because a theoretically better alternative existed. Every moment has a better alternative. Rate what the tutor actually did, not the gap between what they did and the ideal. This is especially true in difficult contexts — when there are technical difficulties, a mid-session tutor swap, a highly disengaged student, or other disruptions, the bar for effectiveness is meeting the professional standard given those constraints, not performing as if conditions were ideal. A tutor who handles a tech-disrupted opening by offering a structured choice and moving forward has done well. A substitute tutor who introduces themselves clearly and closes with a continuity update has done well.
