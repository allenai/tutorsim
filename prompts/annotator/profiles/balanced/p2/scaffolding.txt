### Scaffolding

Scaffolding is temporary, calibrated support that helps a student accomplish a task they cannot yet do independently. It is gradually removed as the student gains competence. The goal is to keep the student in their zone of proximal development -- challenged but not overwhelmed.

Common scaffolding strategies:
- **Breaking down**: Decomposing a complex problem into smaller, manageable steps
- **Hinting**: Providing a cue that points the student toward the answer without giving it away
- **Modeling**: Demonstrating a process or worked example for the student to follow
- **Simplifying**: Reducing the complexity of the task while preserving the core concept
- **Visual/concrete aids**: Using diagrams, manipulatives, or real-world analogies

Over-scaffolding occurs when the tutor provides substantially more support than the student needs -- doing all the cognitive work for them, giving away answers directly, or funneling toward a solution with no student reasoning at any step. Note: mild over-scaffolding (e.g., giving one extra hint, being slightly more directive than necessary) is common in tutoring and does not make an interaction ineffective if the student still engages in thinking. Reserve the label 'over-scaffold' for cases where the student's role was genuinely reduced to passive compliance.

Important nuance about scaffolding strategies: Modeling and direct instruction (explaining a concept, demonstrating a worked example) are legitimate scaffolding strategies, especially when the student explicitly says they don't know how to do something or has just demonstrated a clear gap. A tutor who models a procedure and then has the student apply it is not over-scaffolding — they are teaching. Evaluate scaffolding effectiveness primarily by what the student does AFTER the scaffold: if the student can apply the concept correctly on a subsequent problem, or articulate understanding they didn't have before, the scaffold was effective regardless of how directive the initial explanation was.

### Rigor (Pushing for Depth)

Rigor means maintaining or increasing the cognitive demand on the student. It fosters independence and deeper understanding.

Common rigor strategies:
- **Requiring explanation**: "How did you know that?" or "Why does that work?"
- **Extending problems**: Adding complexity, edge cases, or transfer to new contexts
- **Fading support**: Deliberately removing scaffolds to see if the student can work independently
- **Challenging correct answers**: Asking students to generalize, prove, or find alternative approaches
- **Metacognition prompts**: Asking the student to reflect on their own thinking process

### The Core Tradeoff

At each moment, the tutor decides how much support to provide. Too much support robs the student of productive struggle. Too little support leaves the student floundering. The right choice depends on reading the student's current state -- their understanding, confidence, and engagement.

Note: Not every single correct answer requires a rigor push — a tutor who confirms one answer and moves on is not automatically failing. However, when a student answers multiple problems quickly and correctly with no sign of struggle, and the tutor never asks for reasoning, explanation, or increased challenge across the entire detected range, this pattern of inaction is a meaningful pedagogical gap. The question is not whether any one moment needed a rigor push, but whether the tutor recognized and responded to the student's readiness for more challenge at any point during the detected range.

### Calibration Guidance

When evaluating the result, anchor your assessment on the overall outcome rather than comparing to an ideal.

- A strategy is **effective** if it achieved its primary pedagogical goal: the student engaged in meaningful thinking, corrected a misconception, demonstrated understanding, or made progress. Minor imperfections -- such as the tutor could have asked one more follow-up, could have let the student struggle slightly longer, or provided one hint that wasn't strictly necessary -- do not make an otherwise successful interaction merely partial. Most real tutoring has rough edges; evaluate whether the approach was reasonable and productive, not whether it was optimal. When you find yourself writing 'the core scaffolding was effective, however...' — stop and ask whether the 'however' describes something that visibly harmed the student's learning in THIS interaction, or merely describes a way the moment could have been better. If the latter, the rating should be effective.

- A strategy is **partially effective** if it had meaningful positive impact but also had significant shortcomings that clearly and visibly limited learning during the interaction. Examples: the student got the right answer but clearly doesn't understand why; the tutor addressed part of a misconception but left the core confusion unresolved; the scaffold helped but was substantially over- or under-calibrated in a way that visibly reduced the student's learning opportunity. The shortcoming must be evident in the student's behavior (confusion, passivity, inability to apply the concept) — not merely a hypothetical improvement the tutor could have made. If the student demonstrates understanding and engagement, the interaction is effective even if the tutor's approach was imperfect.

- A strategy is **ineffective** if it failed to achieve its pedagogical goal or was fundamentally misaligned with the student's needs. Examples: the tutor did all the cognitive work and the student passively followed without evidence of understanding; the tutor completely missed the student's actual confusion; the student shows no evidence of learning or engagement; or the tutor missed an obvious and important opportunity with no compensating action.

  Importantly: when a student is clearly coasting — answering quickly, correctly, with no hesitation or challenge — and the tutor simply confirms answers and moves on without ever asking for explanation, reasoning, or increasing difficulty, this IS missing an obvious and important opportunity. Do not soften this to partial merely because the student answered correctly or because the tutor said something encouraging. Correct answers without cognitive challenge are the problem, not evidence against it. Similarly, when a tutor's scaffold is misaligned with the student's actual confusion (e.g., scaffolding computation when the student misunderstands the task), the fact that the tutor tried something does not make the interaction partially effective — rate based on whether the student's actual need was addressed.

Important: When the tutor actively scaffolds (asks questions, gives hints, breaks down the problem) and the student engages in some genuine thinking, this is generally at least partially effective even if the questioning was somewhat directive. However, this floor does NOT apply when the tutor's main action is inaction — simply confirming correct answers and moving on. In those cases, the student's correct answers are not evidence of the tutor's effectiveness; they are evidence that the student was ready for more challenge that the tutor failed to provide. A strategy should be rated ineffective if the student was essentially a passive participant, if the approach was clearly wrong for the situation, or if the tutor failed to act when action was clearly needed.
