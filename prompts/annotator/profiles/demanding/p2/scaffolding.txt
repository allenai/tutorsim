### Scaffolding

Scaffolding is temporary, calibrated support that helps a student accomplish a task they cannot yet do independently. It is gradually removed as the student gains competence. The goal is to keep the student in their zone of proximal development -- challenged but not overwhelmed.

Common scaffolding strategies:
- **Breaking down**: Decomposing a complex problem into smaller, manageable steps
- **Hinting**: Providing a cue that points the student toward the answer without giving it away
- **Modeling**: Demonstrating a process or worked example for the student to follow
- **Simplifying**: Reducing the complexity of the task while preserving the core concept
- **Visual/concrete aids**: Using diagrams, manipulatives, or real-world analogies

Over-scaffolding occurs when the tutor provides more support than the student needs -- doing the cognitive work for them, giving away answers, or funneling toward a solution without letting the student struggle productively.

**Important**: A good initial move (e.g., a well-chosen hint or question) that is immediately undermined by the tutor (e.g., writing the answer before the student can try, narrating all subsequent steps, or abandoning instruction entirely) should be evaluated on its **net impact**, not on the quality of the initial move alone. If the tutor negated their own scaffold — meaning they actively undid its pedagogical value by giving away the answer or doing the thinking — the interaction is not partially effective; it is ineffective. Note: a scaffold that is merely *suboptimal* (e.g., the tutor rephrased when they should have probed) is not the same as a scaffold that is *negated*. Suboptimal scaffolds that still produce some student engagement are typically partial, not ineffective.

### Rigor (Pushing for Depth)

Rigor means maintaining or increasing the cognitive demand on the student. It fosters independence and deeper understanding.

Common rigor strategies:
- **Requiring explanation**: "How did you know that?" or "Why does that work?"
- **Extending problems**: Adding complexity, edge cases, or transfer to new contexts
- **Fading support**: Deliberately removing scaffolds to see if the student can work independently
- **Challenging correct answers**: Asking students to generalize, prove, or find alternative approaches
- **Metacognition prompts**: Asking the student to reflect on their own thinking process
- **Probing errors before correcting**: When a student makes an error, asking "How did you get that?" or "Walk me through your steps" before providing any correction. This reveals the specific misconception and lets the tutor target instruction precisely. Correcting without probing is a significant missed opportunity -- the tutor may address the wrong issue, and the student misses a chance to develop self-monitoring skills.

### The Core Tradeoff

At each moment, the tutor decides how much support to provide. Too much support robs the student of productive struggle. Too little support leaves the student floundering. The right choice depends on reading the student's current state -- their understanding, confidence, and engagement.

## Calibration Guidelines

When writing your result analysis, be precise about what the evidence shows:

- **A correct answer is not proof of learning.** If the tutor walked the student through each step, a correct final answer tells us only that the student can follow instructions. State this clearly rather than treating the correct answer as a positive outcome.
- **Student self-correction is meaningful evidence.** If the student catches and corrects their own error (without the tutor giving it away), this demonstrates genuine understanding. Give this appropriate weight even if other aspects of the interaction were weak.
- **"We don't know" is a valid finding — and it usually indicates partial effectiveness, not ineffectiveness.** If the tutor did not check understanding or provide a transfer problem, the scaffold's effectiveness is uncertain. Do not assume full effectiveness from surface-level compliance. However, distinguish between *uncertain benefit* (the scaffold addressed the right area and the student engaged, but depth of understanding is unverified) and *clear failure* (the tutor did all the thinking, or the student showed no cognitive engagement at all). A scaffold that produces a correct response through some student engagement but without verified understanding is typically partially effective — the uncertainty itself is the weakness, not a reason to dismiss the interaction entirely.
- **Rephrasing is a scaffold, not a giveaway.** When a student says 'I don't understand' and the tutor rephrases the question in simpler or more concrete language, this is a legitimate scaffolding move (simplifying). It differs from giving the answer or narrating steps. If the student then engages with the rephrased question and produces a correct response, the scaffold had *some* positive effect — even if the tutor should ideally have first asked what the student didn't understand. Judge rephrasing on whether the student still had to do reasoning with the rephrased version, not on whether the tutor probed first.
- **Evaluate the whole interaction, not the best moment.** If the tutor had one good question embedded in an otherwise over-scaffolded sequence, the overall interaction should be judged by its dominant character and net impact on student learning.
- **Abandoning instruction is a failure.** If the tutor sets up a scaffold but then gets sidetracked (e.g., off-topic conversation), the scaffold was not partially effective -- it was never completed.
- **Withholding the answer is not the same as scaffolding.** If the tutor avoids giving the answer but the student responds by guessing randomly through options (or trial-and-error without reasoning), the tutor's restraint did not produce cognitive engagement — it produced floundering. In multiple-choice contexts, watch for the student cycling through options without articulating reasoning for their choices. This pattern indicates the scaffold has failed: the student is not doing productive thinking, they are doing elimination by exhaustion. Credit tutor restraint only when the student uses the space to reason, not just to guess.
