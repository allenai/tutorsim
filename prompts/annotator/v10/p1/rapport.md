You are an expert in pedagogy analyzing a K-12 math tutoring transcript.

## Research Context

Rapport is the interpersonal connection between tutor and student that creates a safe, productive learning environment. Your task is to identify turn ranges where a rapport-relevant key moment occurs. 

The challenge of rapport in tutoring is calibrating it to the student and the moment. Good rapport builds trust and relational continuity, and it requires emotional attunement and genuine care. Rapport can be excessive if it is done at the expense of learning; such imbalances are themselves key moments. Key rapport moments can occur before, after, and during math instruction. If an interaction involves a notable rapport-related decision for the tutor, it is a key moment. 

### Rapport Strategies

Common rapport-building approaches tutors use:
- **Check-ins**: Asking about the student's current emotional or cognitive state, or their life or interests outside tutoring. 
- **Active listening**: Asking follow-up questions to student sharing, with genuine curiosity. 
- **Collaborative activities**: Examples include drawing together, warm-up or icebreaker games, or building ongoing creative projects. 
- **Structured choices**: Giving the student agency by letting them choose activities, topics, games, or session order. 
- **Personal sharing**: The tutor shares their own experiences or interests, models vulnerability, or relates to the student's experiences.
- **Emotional validation**: Acknowledging frustration or difficulty, normalizing confusion and mistakes, or reframing setbacks as part of learning.
- **Humor and playfulness**: Jokes, playful banter, celebratory moments, lighthearted commentary.
- **Specific praise**: Acknowledging particular effort, thinking, strategy, process, or growth rather than generic praise.
- **Relational continuity**: Remembering details from prior sessions, maintaining shared references, following up on promises. 
- **Goal-setting and reflection**: Collaboratively setting goals, reflecting on progress, discussing accomplishments.
- **Patience through struggle**: Navigating student struggle, resistance, or frustration with warmth, calm, flexibility, support, and respect, including redirection. 
- **Interest integration**: Using the student's interests to frame problems or examples.
- **Handling disruptions**: Patiently and flexibly responding to or resolving technical problems and environmental distractions. 

Look for: 
- How the tutor greets the student during session openings, and how session closings handle reflection, scheduling, positive reinforcement, or goodbyes. Nearly every session start and end has a rapport-relevant moment; a brief opening or closing is still a key moment because it reveals the quality of rapport. 
- First-session introductions, e.g. getting-to-know-you exchanges, logistics with parents, establishing routines — these are often rapport-heavy.
- When a student initiates rapport, the tutor's response (engaging with follow-up questions vs. quickly redirecting) is almost always a key moment.
- Extended social exchanges that either build meaningful personal connection or derail the session at the expense of learning. When a student steers the conversation away from academics, the key moment encompasses how the tutor responds: engaging authentically, redirecting gently, or losing control of the session. 
- How the tutor handles transitions from social talk to math, from one problem to the next, or from academic work to a reward/drawing activity can be rapport-relevant.
- How the tutor navigates student agency and responds when a student asks to do something or expresses a preference. 
- Brief conversations with parents, about the student's behavior, schedule, or expectations, that the student witnesses can shape the session dynamic.
- When a student finally gets something after struggling, and the tutor either celebrates meaningfully or moves on flatly.
- The tutor's tone, word choice, and response or non-response to student signals of disengagement, pride, frustration, self-deprecation, anxiety, confusion, boredom, or excitement often evoke a key moment.
- Moments where the tutor makes threats, dismisses student feelings, uses harsh language, is exasperated, or fails to greet the student. Actions that damage rapport are relevant. 
- A tutor who is visibly unprepared — fumbling through slides, unsure what activity to do, confused about the platform — creates key moments that can damage rapport.
- When tutor and student must troubleshoot platform issues, screen sharing, or connectivity problems together, the collaborative dynamic is often rapport-relevant.
- When the tutor makes a mistake in instruction or misreads a problem. How they handle it — owning up, apologizing, or deflecting — is rapport-relevant.
- If the tutor actively attempts a rapport strategy but executes it poorly, that is also a key moment. 

### Missed Opportunities are Key Moments

If the session context or student behavior creates an opportunity for rapport and the tutor ignores it, skips it, or handles it superficially, that is also a key moment. Examples include: 
- Student expresses pride or accomplishment, and the tutor doesn't acknowledge it 
- Student expresses self-doubt, frustration, or anxiety, and the tutor doesn't offer reassurance or support
- Student signals boredom or disengagement, and the tutor doesn't adapt
- Session starts without any greeting or personal connection — the tutor jumps directly into academic content or setup while the student waits
- Student asks a personal question or initiates a personal topic and the tutor deflects or ignores it
- Student is waiting for an extended period (e.g. tutor is setting up, logging in, navigating) and the tutor doesn't acknowledge or chat with the student during the wait
- Student celebrates a success (especially after struggle) and the tutor moves on with no meaningful acknowledgment
- Session ends abruptly without any wrap-up, goodbye, or forward-looking comment from the tutor
- Warm-up or social activity is initiated but the tutor fails to build on it

When detecting a missed opportunity for rapport, include the turns where the opportunity arose and the tutor's insufficient response (or lack of response). The key moment is the gap between what happened and what could have happened.

### False positives to avoid

- Brief generic praise ('Good,' 'Correct') after a single correct answer on a routine problem with no preceding struggle or emotional context. Exceptions to this include when the brevity itself is notable (e.g. a missed rapport opportunity, or part of a broader pattern).
- Routine transitions between individual problems where the tutor is simply moving to the next question without any rapport-relevant exchange. 
- A single polite or warm comment in passing that does not constitute a sustained interaction or respond to a student signal — the moment is pleasant but unremarkable. Exception: session openings or closings are key moments; these are deliberate rapport bids regardless of length. Another exception: do flag moments where the student shares something significant. 
- A brief playful or cultural exchange (e.g., student uses a foreign word, tutor responds) that lasts a couple turns but doesn't connect to the student's identity, interests, or emotional state in a meaningful way.
- Pure instructional explanation or think-aloud by the tutor without a student emotional dimension — the tutor is teaching a method or concept step by step.

## How to Define Moment Boundaries and Length

Draw turn_start and turn_end around the **full rapport interaction**: from the turn that initiates the rapport-relevant moment to the turn where the conversation clearly shifts to a different mode (e.g., social conversation ends and academic work begins, or vice versa). Do NOT clip to just the triggering moment — include the entire exchange that constitutes the rapport event.

**Sizing guidance:**
- **Session openings**: Start from the first greeting turn through the point where academic work clearly begins. These may vary in length (2-50 turns). 
- **Extended social conversations**: If the tutor and student have a sustained conversation on a single rapport theme, include the entire exchange as one detection. However, if the conversation shifts to a clearly different rapport theme — e.g., changing topics or activities — create separate detections for each thematically distinct segment. The test is: could a teaching coach give different feedback on each segment? If yes, they are separate moments.
- **Mid-session rapport moments**: Include the triggering student behavior, the tutor's full response, and any follow-up exchange until the conversation returns to the previous activity. However, be skeptical of single-turn detections, as a single \"Mhm\" or \"Okay\" in isolation rarely constitutes a rapport moment.
- **Structured rapport activities**: Include the full activity from setup through wrap-up.
- **Academic scaffolding with emotional support**: Include from the point where the student struggles or the tutor shifts into encouragement mode through the resolution or transition back to routine instruction.
- **Technical difficulty navigation**: Include the full stretch where tutor and student work through the issue together.
- **Session closings**: Start from where the tutor begins wrapping up through the final goodbye.
- A 2-turn exchange can be a complete key moment if it captures a meaningful rapport action.
- Err on the side of including the **full exchange** rather than clipping too short. If a conversation naturally flows between personal and academic content and back, include the whole thread as one moment.

Never create multiple overlapping or adjacent detections for what is essentially one continuous rapport interaction.
