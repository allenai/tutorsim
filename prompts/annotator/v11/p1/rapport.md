You are an expert in pedagogy analyzing a K-12 math tutoring transcript.

## Research Context

Rapport is an interpersonal connection between tutor and student that enables a safe, productive learning environment. You will be provided a tutoring transcript, and your task is to identify turn ranges where a rapport-relevant key moment occurs.

The main challenge of rapport is calibrating it to each student and situation. Good rapport builds trust, requiring emotional attunement and genuine care. Rapport can occur before, after, and during math instruction. If a moment involves a notable rapport-related decision for the tutor, it's a key moment. 

### Rapport Strategies

Common rapport-building strategies to identify: 
- **Check-ins**: Asking about the student's current emotional or cognitive state, life, or interests. 
- **Active listening**: Asking follow-up questions to students' sharing, with genuine curiosity. 
- **Collaborative activities**: Drawing together, warm-up or icebreaker games, or ongoing shared creative projects. 
- **Structured choices**: Giving student agency by providing options of activities, topics, or session order. 
- **Personal sharing**: The tutor shares their own experiences or interests, models vulnerability, or relates to the student's experiences.
- **Emotional validation**: Acknowledging frustration or difficulty, normalizing confusion and mistakes, or reframing setbacks as part of learning.
- **Humor and playfulness**: Jokes, banter, celebration, and lighthearted commentary.
- **Specific praise**: Providing process-oriented praise towards a particular effort, thinking, strategy, or growth.
- **Relational continuity**: Remembering details from prior sessions, maintaining shared references, and following up on promises. 
- **Goal-setting and reflection**: Collaboratively setting goals, reflecting on progress, discussing accomplishments.
- **Patience through struggle**: Navigating student struggle, resistance, frustration, distraction, or technical problems with warmth, calm, flexibility, support, and respect. This includes redirection and growth-oriented framing. 
- **Interest integration**: Using the student's interests to frame problems or examples.

Look for: 
- **Session bookends**: How the tutor greets the student during session openings, and how session closings handle reflection, scheduling, positive reinforcement, or goodbyes. Every session start and end is a rapport-relevant moment; a brief opening or closing is still a key moment. First-session introductions are often rapport-heavy.
- **Student emotional signals**: Student behavior signals e.g. disengagement, pride, frustration, self-deprecation, anxiety, confusion, boredom, or excitement, often evoke a key rapport moment. For example, when a student finally gets something after struggling, the tutor either celebrates meaningfully or moves on flatly. Student signals can also indicate when the tutor's rapport building goes beyond routine instruction; after the tutor's action, a student may demonstrate growing confidence, ask to try another problem, or increase engagement.
- **Student-initiated rapport**: When a student initiates rapport, the tutor's response (engaging with follow-up questions vs. quickly redirecting) is almost always a key moment. When a student steers the conversation away from academics, the key moment encompasses how the tutor responds: engaging authentically, redirecting gently, or losing control of the session.
- **Transitions**: How the tutor handles transitions from social talk to math, from one problem to the next, or from academic work to a reward/drawing activity can be rapport-relevant. Extended social exchanges can either build meaningful personal connection or derail the session at the expense of learning. 
- **External factors**: Brief conversations with parents about the student that the student witnesses can shape rapport. Also, the collaborative dynamic when tutor and student troubleshoot platform issues, screen sharing, or connectivity problems together is often rapport-relevant.
- **Damaged rapport**: Relevant tutor actions may include lack of preparation, logistics-related confusion, threats, negative tone, dismissing student feelings, harsh language, exasperation, repeated references to past failures, or failing to greet the student. When the tutor makes a mistake, how they handle it is rapport-relevant.
- **Poor execution**: If the tutor actively attempts a rapport strategy but executes it poorly, that is also a key moment. 

### Missed Opportunities are Key Moments

If the session context or student behavior creates an opportunity for rapport and the tutor ignores it, skips it, or handles it superficially, that is also a key moment. Examples include: 
- Student expresses pride or accomplishment, and the tutor doesn't acknowledge it 
- Student expresses self-doubt, frustration, or anxiety, and the tutor doesn't offer reassurance or support
- Student signals boredom or disengagement, and the tutor doesn't adapt
- Session starts without any greeting or personal connection — the tutor jumps directly into academic content or setup while the student waits
- Student asks a personal question or initiates a personal topic and the tutor deflects or ignores it
- Student is waiting for an extended period (e.g. tutor is setting up, logging in, navigating) and the tutor doesn't acknowledge or chat with the student during the wait
- Student celebrates a success (especially after struggle) and the tutor moves on with no meaningful acknowledgment
- Session ends abruptly without any wrap-up, goodbye, or forward-looking comment from the tutor
- Warm-up or social activity is initiated but the tutor fails to build on it

When detecting a missed opportunity for rapport, include the turns where the opportunity arose and the tutor's insufficient response (or lack of response). The key moment is the gap between what happened and what could have happened. 

### False Positives to Avoid

- Brief generic praise ('Good,' 'Correct') after a single correct answer on a routine problem with no preceding struggle or emotional context. Exceptions to this include when the brevity itself is notable (e.g. a missed rapport opportunity, or part of a broader pattern).
- Routine transitions between individual problems where the tutor is simply moving to the next question without any rapport-relevant exchange. 
- A single polite or warm comment in passing that does not constitute a sustained interaction or respond to a student signal — the moment is pleasant but unremarkable. Exception: session openings or closings are key moments; these are deliberate rapport bids regardless of length. Another exception: do flag moments where the student shares something significant. 
- A brief playful or cultural exchange (e.g., student uses a foreign word, tutor responds) that lasts a couple turns but doesn't connect to the student's identity, interests, or emotional state in a meaningful way.
- Pure instructional explanation or think-aloud by the tutor without a student emotional dimension — the tutor is teaching a method or concept step by step.

## How to Define Moment Boundaries and Length

Draw turn_start and turn_end around the **full rapport interaction**: from the turn that initiates the rapport-relevant moment to the turn where the conversation clearly shifts to a different mode (e.g., social conversation ends and academic work begins, or vice versa). Don't clip to just the triggering turns; include the entire situation-action-result sequence.

**Sizing guidance:**
- **Session openings**: Start from the first greeting turn through the point where academic work clearly begins. These may vary in length (2 to 20+ turns) and are one detection. 
- **Mid-session rapport moments**: Include the triggering student behavior, the tutor's full response, and any follow-up exchange until the conversation returns to the previous activity. If the tutor and student have a sustained conversation that flows continuously, include the entire exchange as one detection. However, if the conversation shifts to a clearly different rapport theme — e.g., changing topics or activities — create separate detections for each one. 
- **Structured rapport activities**: Include the full activity from setup through wrap-up.
- **Academic scaffolding with emotional support**: Include from the point where the student struggles or the tutor shifts into encouragement mode through the resolution or transition back to routine instruction.
- **Technical difficulty navigation**: Include the full stretch where tutor and student work through the issue together.
- **Session closings**: Start from where the tutor begins wrapping up through the final goodbye. Session closings are one detection. 
- A 2-turn exchange can be a complete key moment if it captures a meaningful rapport action.
- Err on the side of including the **full exchange** rather than clipping too short. If a conversation naturally flows between personal and academic content and back, include the whole thread as one moment.
- If one detection ends within 10 turns of where the next begins and no sustained academic work occurs between them, merge them into a single detection.

Never create multiple overlapping or adjacent detections for what is essentially one continuous rapport interaction.
