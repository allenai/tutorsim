## Research Context

Rapport is the interpersonal connection between tutor and student that creates a safe, productive learning environment. For each flagged moment, you will analyze the situation, the tutor's strategy, and how well it worked.

The challenge of rapport in tutoring is calibrating it to the student and the moment. Good rapport builds trust and relational continuity, and it requires emotional attunement and genuine care. 

### Rapport Strategies

Common rapport-building approaches tutors use:
- **Check-ins**: Asking about the student's current emotional or cognitive state, life, or interests. 
- **Active listening**: Asking follow-up questions to students' sharing, with genuine curiosity. 
- **Collaborative activities**: Drawing together, warm-up or icebreaker games, or ongoing shared creative projects. 
- **Structured choices**: Giving student agency by providing options of activities, topics, or session order. 
- **Personal sharing**: The tutor shares their own experiences or interests, models vulnerability, or relates to the student's experiences.
- **Emotional validation**: Acknowledging frustration or difficulty, normalizing confusion and mistakes, or reframing setbacks as part of learning.
- **Humor and playfulness**: Jokes, banter, celebration, and lighthearted commentary.
- **Specific praise**: Providing process-oriented praise towards a particular effort, thinking, strategy, or growth.
- **Relational continuity**: Remembering details from prior sessions, maintaining shared references, and following up on promises. 
- **Goal-setting and reflection**: Collaboratively setting goals, reflecting on progress, discussing accomplishments.
- **Patience through struggle**: Navigating student struggle, resistance, frustration, distraction, or technical problems with warmth, calm, flexibility, support, and respect. This includes redirection and growth-oriented framing. 
- **Interest integration**: Using the student's interests to frame problems or examples.

Patient, supportive instruction can strengthen the tutor-student relationship even without a deliberate rapport strategy. When assessing such moments, consider whether the tutor's approach demonstrated emotional attunement beyond regular instruction. Also, the same strategy can be effective or ineffective depending on context.

## Considerations

When assessing whether a situation is appropriate for rapport-building and the effectiveness of a tutor's action, consider the following. 

**Student Behavior**
- If a student expresses excitement, pride, vulnerability, self-doubt, or frustration, the moment is a rapport-building opportunity. 
- More effective signs include: the student elaborating, reciprocating the tutor's sharing, initiating non-academic conversation, volunteering personal stories, referencing prior shared experiences, persisting through difficulty after encouragement, becoming more engaged or confident over the interaction, thanking the tutor, or comfortably expressing vulnerability. 
- Less effective signs include: the student giving one-word answers, reducing their engagement, ignoring praise or encouragement, actively resisting the tutor's rapport attempts, showing discomfort, or following instructions without genuine interpersonal connection. 
- Consider the student's baseline: some students are naturally quieter or less expressive. 
- Effective rapport is bidirectional; monologues are weaker.

**Timing & Duration**
When and how long rapport occurs matters as much as the strategy itself:
- Session openings are a natural time for check-ins, personal questions, and warm-ups.
- Session endings are appropriate for reflection, praise, previewing next sessions, and personal conversation. 
- Natural pauses between problem sets, topics, or activities are good opportunities for brief rapport.
- Rapport that crowds out instruction is problematic, especially mid-session. Activities exceeding 20 minutes are frequently rated as partially effective at best, as engagement does not compensate for the opportunity cost. However, in first sessions, early sessions, or when the student is clearly engaged and building trust, longer rapport-building is more acceptable.
- A single brief tangent is acceptable; multiple or prolonged tangents in the same session suggest the tutor is failing to maintain productive session structure. 
- Tech setup and dead time can double as rapport opportunities. 
- Context can modify timing expectations: a student undergoing a significant event may warrant more rapport time than usual.

**Effective vs. Ineffective Patterns**

Examples of moments that are perceived as effective: 
- When the student gives a minimal response (e.g. 'fine'), an effective tutor pivots: asking a specific, open-ended follow-up, trying a new angle, or sharing something to invite reciprocity.
- The tutor demonstrates care by remembering a specific detail from a prior session and following up on it. They may promise to return to a topic later and actually follow through, or collaboratively make plans and goals for next time with the student.
- Questions, praise, adjustments, and comments are specific to the student and varied, not formulaic. 
- When the student expresses struggle, anxiety, frustration, or self-doubt, the tutor is emotionally attuned: they may acknowledge the feeling, normalize difficulty, provide specific encouragement, and/or show patience. This is especially effective if the student demonstrates a change in engagement or confidence. 
- When a student shares something personal unprompted or goes on a brief tangent, the tutor engages warmly for a few minutes with follow-up questions, and then smoothly redirects back to work. 

Examples of moments that are perceived as ineffective: 
- When a student is bidding for the tutor's attention, e.g. talking about their day, showing a project, asking questions, and the tutor does not respond, acknowledge, or engage, this is ineffective.  
- When a tutor is visibly unprepared and flustered, or they spend time navigating technology in silence while the student waits with no interaction - this may erode rapport. 
- If a flagged moment seems to contain no rapport-building action, say so and assess whether it was a missed opportunity given the context. No rapport is usually ineffective rapport. 
- The tutor uses punitive language or tone in response to student mistakes, tells the student discouraging or confidence-damaging information, or makes them feel like a burden. 
- Ineffective rapport includes inappropriate conversational topics (e.g. politics, tutor complaints). 
- The tutor provides over-the-top praise of innate ability rather than focusing on the student's effort, strategy, or reasoning. Though generic praise may be viewed as effective by some teachers, repeatedly reusing the same praise regardless of difficulty or process is less effective.
- The tutor ends the session with only logistics without celebrating progress, reflecting on the session, or giving the student a moment to share their experience.  
- When the student shares something or expresses an emotion that warrants rapport, it is a missed opportunity if the tutor instead redirects or immediately moves on to problem-solving or logistics.
- The conversation is one-sided: the student may talk for many turns without the tutor meaningfully contributing, or the tutor may talk at the student for an extended stretch without checking for understanding, asking questions, or giving the student a chance to respond.

### Calibrating Mixed Assessments

Sometimes evidence is genuinely mixed with competing considerations. Present both sides, acknowledge what worked and what did not, and describe the result as partially effective. Common patterns that provoke mixed assessments include: 
- The tutor asks appropriate check-in questions at the right time, but the student responds minimally and the tutor does not adapt or probe further. A student giving brief or one-word responses does not automatically make a rapport strategy ineffective. Some students are reserved - weigh response patterns in context. A tutor's effort to connect still has value. 
- The tutor may use a well-intentioned, sound rapport technique, but at a suboptimal moment.
- The tutor may demonstrate one effective element, but then mishandle the moment or fail to build on it meaningfully.  
- The student may enjoy an interaction and the rapport-building is genuine, but it occurs at the cost of instruction time. Assess effectiveness by considering timing, duration, the genuine connection made or reinforced, and whether the tutor eventually redirects to academics. 

Moments are likely effective if tutor's strategy is pedagogically sound and well-timed, and the student responds in a way that suggests connection was built. Moments are likely ineffective if they involve missed opportunities, rapport-damaging tutor actions, and miscalibrated tutor decisions.
